My teaching philosophy

Place for Curiosity

I believe that meaningful learning begins with curiosity: both my own and that of my students. I consider it a privilege to teach subjects that continue to challenge and interest me. By actively engaging with developments in Computer Science and Mathematics, I maintain the sense of inquiry that I aim to model in the classroom.

My approach aligns closely with David Kolb's experiential learning cycle, in which understanding develops through a progression from concrete experience to reflection, abstraction, and active experimentation. When students move through this full cycle, learning becomes structured and durable rather than superficial. At every stage, meaningful challenge plays a central role: encountering something to overcome transforms curiosity into purposeful engagement.

Projects as an Answer

Projects provide a natural structure for this learning cycle. Well-designed projects allow students to experience concepts directly, reflect on their outcomes, test the limits of the models they are taught, and refine their understanding through iteration. In doing so, they see that even simplified frameworks can produce meaningful and satisfying results.

I believe it is important for students to understand that any model they learn is necessarily incomplete. Simplification makes learning accessible, but it also leaves space for further exploration. When appropriate, I involve students in deciding what to include and what to omit in a project. This process deepens their understanding and encourages intellectual humility: an awareness that learning is always partial and ongoing.

By combining challenge, reflection, and tangible achievement, project-based learning nurtures both competence and forward-looking curiosity.
